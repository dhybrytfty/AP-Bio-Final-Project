What is Biotechnology?

Biotechnology encompasses many different fields, but it can generally be defined as the application of biological science and engineering to create useful products such as vaccines, medicines, and food.

Biotechnology in the Modern World

Modern biotechnology uses living systems and biological processes to solve medical, agricultural, and industrial problems.

Biotechnology is now involved in medicine, food production, genetics, pharmaceuticals, and scientific research.

Medicine & Healthcare

Vaccines, cancer treatments, genetic testing, gene editing like CRISPR, and making insulin using bacteria, are all part of biotechnology in modern medicine.

Agriculture

Genetically modified crops, pest-resistant plants, better crop yields, and foods with improved nutrition quality are all examples of biotechnology in agriculture.

Industrial Biotechnology

Biotechnology is used to make biofuels, biodegradable plastics, and enzymes that help produce materials in cleaner and more efficient ways.

Genetics & Research

Scientists use DNA sequencing, CRISPR, cloning, and stem cell research to study genes and understand how living things work at a deeper level.

Origins of Biotechnology

Biotech Timeline

~9500–10,000 BC — Domestication of crops

Domestication started around 10,000 to 12,000 years ago in the Fertile Crescent, which includes parts of Iraq, Iran, Syria, and Turkey. Some of the earliest domesticated species were barley, chickpeas, lentils, and flax. This is considered to be the earliest example of biotechnology, as humans took natural biological processes, such as selective pressures, and manipulated them to create desirable outputs, such as more favorable crops.

1859

Charles Darwin published On the Origin of Species by Means of Natural Selection, which offered a “plausible and compelling explanation for how species evolve” (Darwin, 2009). Darwin argued that all existing creatures descended from progenitor species and that the evolution of species is due to random variations in organisms’ phenotype and that some of these variations better equip creatures to survive their environment. While he did not know what caused these so-called “variations”, which turned out to be genetic mutations, this concept is foundational to biotechnology. Selection based on traits occurs often in biotechnology, such as antibiotic resistance in bacterial cultures or artificial selection of crops.

1865

Gregor Mendel (1822-1884) presented his theory on inheritance to the Natural Science Society in Brunn, Austria. Based on his work with the pea plant (Pisum sativum), Mendel “proposed that invisible internal units of information account for observable traits” (Verma et al., 2011). These units were later found to be genes. Understanding the concept of genetics and inheritance is essential for modern-day biotechnology.

1868

Friedrich Miescher (1844-1895) discovered and named nuclein, a compound made of nucleic acid, extracted from pus. This discovery, combined with others, “became the basis of modern molecular biology” (Verma et al., 2011).

1882

Walther Hesse first used agar in a lab setting. Since then, agar has become “the most acceptable and useful medium to obtain pure microbial cultures” (Verma et al., 2011).

1919

The term biotechnology was used for the first time by Karl Erkey, a Hungarian Engineer.

1928

Alexander Fleming discovered antibiotics, eventually isolating penicillin from Penicillium notatum mold. Antibiotics have saved countless lives and are all but necessary in this day and age. They are used very frequently both in medicine and in biotechnology.

1953

DNA structure discovered. James Watson and Francis Crick, using data from Rosalind Franklin and Maurice Wilkins, discovered the double-helix structure of DNA. This opened the proverbial Pandora’s box of genetics, allowing scientists to understand and eventually manipulate genetics.

2000

Human Genome Project completed.

2012

Jennifer Doudna and Emmanuelle Charpentier invented CRISPR-Cas9 gene editing technology. This invention allows researchers to edit DNA with “unprecedented precision and efficiency” (Jennifer).

2020

mRNA vaccines were produced in response to the COVID-19 pandemic. Using cutting-edge biotechnology, the production of vaccines was expedited. Unlike traditional vaccines, mRNA vaccines are created by taking a target gene sequence, such as the spike protein in COVID-19, and inserting it into a plasmid. This genetic material can then be rapidly amplified (often using PCR). This allows “new mRNA vaccines to be tested and distributed rapidly” (COVID-19, 2021). The COVID-19 vaccines were, overall, a success; their success is a shining example of what biotechnology can do for the world.
